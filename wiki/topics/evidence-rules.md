## Definition

Evidence law governs what facts a court may receive, how they are proved, and the weight to be given to different modes of proof. The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA) replaced the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 with effect from 1 July 2024. The BSA preserves most of the 1872 Act's structure but modernises provisions on electronic evidence and secondary evidence.

## Current Law

**Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA)**

- **§ 2(1)(c)** — "Document" includes electronic records, server logs, emails, digital audio/video, and messages stored in devices *(expanded from Evidence Act)*
- **§ 4** — May presume / Shall presume / Conclusive proof: three levels of presumption
- **§ 8** — Facts in issue and relevant facts: only relevant facts are admissible
- **§ 9** — Facts connected with facts in issue (occasion, cause, effect, opportunity, motive) are relevant
- **§ 17** — Admissions: statements made by a party to the suit against their interest
- **§ 22** — Confessions to police: not provable against the accused; confession made in police custody only admissible if made in immediate presence of a Magistrate
- **§ 23** — Confession by co-accused: can be taken into consideration but is not substantive evidence against others
- **§ 26** — Dying declaration: statement made by person who believes death is imminent, concerning cause of death — admissible even if person survives
- **§ 39** — Relevancy of judgments: previous judgments relevant in certain circumstances (res judicata, estoppel)
- **§ 57** — Documents admissible in evidence: primary evidence (original document) vs secondary evidence (copies)
- **§ 61** — Proof of electronic records: electronic record admissible as documentary evidence; governed by §§ 62-63
- **§ 63** — Certificate for electronic evidence *(replaces § 65B Indian Evidence Act)*: electronic record is admissible if accompanied by a certificate from the person responsible for the device/computer — certifying hash value, operating conditions, software used. Certificate under § 63 is now mandatory for admissibility of electronic records
- **§ 67** — Proof of signature: if signature is disputed, court may compare handwriting or obtain expert opinion
- **§ 113** — Burden of proof generally on person asserting a fact
- **§ 116** — Presumption of innocence in criminal cases: prosecution must prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- **§ 117** — Estoppel: person who makes a representation cannot deny it in proceedings where another has acted on it

## Key Provisions

**Primary vs secondary evidence:**

| Type | Meaning | When admissible |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| Primary | Original document | Always |
| Secondary | Certified copy, copy from original | When original lost/destroyed/in custody of opponent/court |

**Electronic records — § 63 certificate (new BSA):** The Supreme Court's pre-BSA jurisprudence on § 65B Indian Evidence Act (Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v Kailash Kushanrao, 2020) required a § 65B certificate at the time of production. BSA § 63 codifies this: a certificate from a responsible official of the device or server is mandatory. Without the certificate, electronic records are inadmissible.

**Confession vs admission:**
- Admission: any party, civil or criminal, any relevant statement
- Confession: only in criminal cases; made by accused; must be voluntary and not to a police officer

**Dying declaration (§ 26 BSA):** Need not be made to a Magistrate; corroboration not mandatory; can be sole basis for conviction if court is satisfied it was voluntary and truthful.

**Hostile witness (§ 167 BSA):** If a prosecution witness turns hostile, the party who called the witness may cross-examine them. Hostile witness evidence does not automatically become inadmissible — court weighs what is reliable.

**Expert opinion (§ 39 BSA):** Expert on foreign law, science, art, identity of handwriting or finger impressions — opinion is relevant. Expert is not a conclusive authority; court forms its own opinion.

**Privileged communications:**
- § 126 BSA: Legal professional privilege — advocate cannot disclose communications from client
- § 129 BSA: Communications during marriage — spouse cannot be compelled to disclose
- § 128 BSA: Official communications — State can claim privilege for documents against public interest

**Standard of proof:**
- Criminal: Beyond reasonable doubt (§ 116 BSA)
- Civil: Balance of probabilities
- Some statutory cases: Reverse burden on accused (NDPS § 54, PMLA § 24, POCSO § 29)
