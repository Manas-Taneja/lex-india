## Definition

A tort is a civil wrong (other than breach of contract) for which a court provides a remedy in the form of damages. India has no codified general tort law; the common law of torts developed by courts (following English common law with Indian modifications) governs. Negligence is the primary tort — failure to take reasonable care causing foreseeable harm. Key statutory torts include the Motor Vehicles Act, 1988 (no-fault compensation) and the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (service deficiency).

## Current Law

**Common law of torts (judge-made law)**

- **Donoghue v Stevenson principle (adopted in India):** Duty of care owed to neighbours — persons so closely and directly affected by one's act that one ought reasonably to have them in contemplation
- **Elements of negligence:** (1) Duty of care; (2) Breach of that duty; (3) Causation (factual and proximate); (4) Damage suffered
- **Res ipsa loquitur:** The thing speaks for itself — where the accident is of a kind that does not ordinarily occur without negligence, and the instrumentality was in defendant's control, negligence is inferred
- **Strict liability (Rylands v Fletcher):** Person who brings onto their land anything likely to do mischief if it escapes is liable for all damage that is the natural consequence of its escape; no requirement to prove negligence
- **Absolute liability (M.C. Mehta v Union of India, 1987 — Supreme Court of India):** For enterprises engaged in hazardous or inherently dangerous activities — liability is absolute and non-delegable; no exceptions (unlike Rylands v Fletcher); compensation commensurate with magnitude and capacity of enterprise

**Motor Vehicles Act, 1988**

- **§ 140** — No-fault liability: owner of motor vehicle liable to pay compensation for death (₹50,000) or grievous hurt (₹25,000) regardless of fault — payable as interim relief
- **§ 163A** — Structured formula (Second Schedule): compensation for death or permanent disablement based on age and income formula; no fault needs to be proved; replaced by ₹5 lakh minimum for death (2019 amendment)
- **§ 166** — Claims petition: claim filed before Motor Accidents Claims Tribunal (MACT); tribunal decides on fault and quantum
- **§ 168** — Award of compensation: just compensation; tribunal follows multiplier method for permanent disability and death
- **§ 196** — Compulsory third-party insurance: motor vehicle cannot be driven without third-party liability insurance
- **Multiplier method (General Manager KSRTC v Susamma Thomas, 1994):** Annual dependency loss × multiplier based on age of deceased = compensation. Standardised by Supreme Court in Sarla Verma (2009) and National Insurance v Pranay Sethi (2017) — 25% addition to income for future prospects (for those below 40).

**Consumer Protection Act, 2019**

- **§ 2(47)** — Unfair trade practice: false representation, misleading advertisement, deceptive pricing
- **§ 2(11)** — Deficiency: failure/imperfection/shortcoming in service standard, quality, or performance
- **§ 17** — Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA): investigates, inquires, and orders recalls; bans misleading advertisements
- **§ 34-58** — Consumer disputes redressal commissions: District (≤ ₹50 lakh), State (₹50 lakh–₹2 crore), National (> ₹2 crore)
- **§ 39** — Reliefs: refund, replacement, removal of defect, compensation, punitive damages, discontinuation of unfair practice

## Key Provisions

**Negligence vs strict liability vs absolute liability:**

| Doctrine | Fault required | Defences available | Origin |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|--------|
| Negligence | Yes — breach of duty of care | Contributory negligence, volenti, act of God | Common law |
| Strict liability (Rylands v Fletcher) | No | Act of God, plaintiff's default, act of third party, statutory authority | English common law adopted in India |
| Absolute liability (MC Mehta) | No | **None** | Supreme Court of India 1987 |

**Vicarious liability:** Employer liable for torts committed by employee in the course of employment. Hospital liable for negligence of doctors on its rolls. Government liable for torts of its servants (Post & Telegraph v Laxmi Bai, 1965 — partially; full constitutional tort liability recognised post-Nilabati Behera v State of Orissa, 1993).

**Constitutional tort (Art 21):** State can be liable for violation of fundamental rights — compensation awarded under writ jurisdiction. Nilabati Behera and D.K. Basu laid down that custodial death and unlawful detention attract monetary compensation in writ proceedings.

**Medical negligence — standard:** Indian Medical Association v V.P. Shantha (1995) — doctors are "service providers" under consumer law; medical negligence = failure to exercise ordinary skill expected of a reasonably competent medical professional in that field (Bolam test modified by Indian courts).

**Defamation (tort):** False statement of fact published to a third party, causing reputational harm. In India, defamation is both a civil wrong (damages) and a criminal offence (BNS § 356). Truth is a complete defence in civil defamation; qualified privilege for statements made in discharge of duty.

**Contributory negligence:** Damages reduced in proportion to plaintiff's own negligence. India follows apportionment (not the older all-or-nothing rule).

**Limitation for tort suits:** Generally 3 years from date of cause of action (Art 113, Limitation Act). For tort cases continuing over time (nuisance, trespass), each fresh act restarts limitation.

**No codified Torts Act:** Bills have been proposed but not enacted. Courts continue to apply evolving common law supplemented by specific statutes (Motor Vehicles, Consumer Protection, Environment Protection, etc.).
