## Definition

Appeal and revision are the primary mechanisms for challenging court decisions in India. An appeal is a statutory right to have a decision reconsidered by a higher court on facts and law. Revision is a supervisory jurisdiction exercised by a superior court over subordinate courts when no appeal lies or to correct jurisdictional errors. The right to appeal does not exist unless expressly conferred by statute.

## Current Law

**Criminal appeals — Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)**

- **§ 415** — Appeal from order of acquittal: State or complainant may appeal; complainant requires special leave from High Court; State may appeal as of right
- **§ 416** — Appeal against conviction: convicted person may appeal to Sessions Court (if convicted by Magistrate) or to High Court (if convicted by Sessions Court)
- **§ 418** — No appeal in petty cases: no appeal where fine ≤ ₹100 or sentence ≤ 1 month (summary trial), unless conviction overturned on appeal
- **§ 419** — Appeal to High Court from Sessions Court
- **§ 421** — Appeal to Supreme Court: under Art 136 Constitution (Special Leave Petition) or Art 132-134 (certificate cases)
- **§ 438** — Revision by Sessions Court or High Court: call for records of any inferior court; examine legality/regularity/propriety of finding/sentence/order; may alter, annul, reverse, or modify
- **§ 442** — No revision against interlocutory orders
- **§ 444** — Reference to High Court: Magistrate/Sessions Court may refer a question of law to High Court for opinion

**Civil appeals — Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC)**

- **§ 96** — First appeal from original decree: lies to District Court (from lower courts) or High Court (from District Court); on facts and law
- **§ 100** — Second appeal to High Court: only on a substantial question of law; no re-appreciation of evidence
- **§ 104** — Appeal from orders (as distinct from decrees): listed orders only
- **§ 109** — Appeal to Supreme Court: on certificate from High Court (Art 133/134) or Special Leave (Art 136)
- **§ 115** — Revision by High Court: where subordinate court acted without/in excess of jurisdiction, or failed to exercise jurisdiction, or acted illegally/with material irregularity
- **§ 114** — Review of judgment: same court may review its own decree on discovery of new evidence, mistake apparent on the face of record, or any other sufficient reason

**Constitutional jurisdiction:**
- **Art 226** — High Court writ jurisdiction: writs of habeas corpus, mandamus, certiorari, prohibition, quo warranto against State and others
- **Art 32** — Supreme Court writ jurisdiction: to enforce fundamental rights
- **Art 136** — Special Leave Petition (SLP): Supreme Court may grant special leave to appeal from any judgment of any court/tribunal in India

## Key Provisions

**Appeal hierarchy — criminal:**

| Conviction by | First appeal | Second appeal |
|--------------|-------------|---------------|
| Magistrate First Class | Sessions Court | High Court (revision) |
| Sessions Court | High Court | Supreme Court (SLP/certificate) |
| High Court (original) | Supreme Court | — |

**Appeal hierarchy — civil:**

| Decree by | First appeal | Second appeal | Further |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Munsiff / Civil Judge | District Court | High Court (LoL only) | SC (SLP) |
| District Court | High Court | — | SC (SLP) |
| High Court (original) | Division Bench | — | SC |

**Substantial question of law (§ 100 CPC):** In second civil appeal, only questions of law — not facts — can be examined. High Court cannot reappreciate witness credibility or overturn findings of fact unless there is a perversity.

**Revision vs appeal:** Revision does not entitle the court to substitute its own decision as an appellate court would. It only corrects jurisdictional defects, illegality, or material irregularities. Revision lies against orders, not just decrees.

**Delay condonation:** Under the Limitation Act, 1963 (§ 5), appeals filed after the period may be condoned if the appellant shows sufficient cause. Supreme Court in Collector Land Acquisition v Mst Katiji (1987) — liberal approach to condonation.

**No revision against acquittal by lower court in revision:** Prosecution cannot use revision to circumvent the more stringent appeal-from-acquittal route.

**Letters Patent Appeal (LPA):** In High Courts with original jurisdiction (Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, Delhi), an appeal lies from a single judge to a Division Bench under the Letters Patent.

**SLP (Art 136):** The Supreme Court's widest jurisdiction — can admit an SLP against any judgment. Court grants leave selectively; refusal of SLP does not mean the lower court was correct.
