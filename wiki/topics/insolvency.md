## Definition

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 (IBC) is the primary law governing insolvency resolution and bankruptcy in India. It replaced a fragmented regime spread across multiple laws (Companies Act, SICA, Presidency Towns Insolvency Act, etc.) and created a time-bound, creditor-driven resolution process. IBC covers corporate insolvency resolution (CIRP), fast-track insolvency, voluntary liquidation, personal insolvency (individuals and firms), and fresh start process. The National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) is the adjudicating authority for corporate persons.

## Current Law

**Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016**

- **§ 3(12)** — Default: non-payment of whole or part of debt when it becomes due and payable; minimum default threshold ₹1 crore (increased from ₹1 lakh by 2020 notification — COVID relief measure retained)
- **§ 6** — Who may initiate CIRP: financial creditor (§ 7), operational creditor (§ 9), or corporate debtor itself (§ 10)
- **§ 7** — Application by financial creditor: financial creditor or class of financial creditors files before NCLT with evidence of default; NCLT must admit or reject within 14 days
- **§ 9** — Application by operational creditor: demand notice → 10 days for payment → if no response or dispute raised → application to NCLT
- **§ 13** — Declaration of moratorium: NCLT orders moratorium on admission — all suits/proceedings against corporate debtor stayed; no new suits; no enforcement of security interests; no alienation of assets
- **§ 16** — Appointment of Interim Resolution Professional (IRP): appointed within 14 days; takes over management from Board of Directors
- **§ 21** — Committee of Creditors (CoC): financial creditors form CoC; voting rights proportional to financial debt; operational creditors excluded from CoC but have representation rights
- **§ 22** — Resolution Professional (RP): IRP confirmed or replaced by CoC; RP manages corporate debtor during CIRP
- **§ 25** — Duties of RP: collects and presents information memorandum; invites resolution plans; conducts CIRP
- **§ 30** — Submission of resolution plan: any person (except undischarged insolvent, wilful defaulter, promoter with NPA ≥ 1 year) may submit plan; must provide for minimum payment to operational creditors (not less than liquidation value)
- **§ 31** — Approval of resolution plan: CoC approves with 66% majority; NCLT approves if compliant; plan binding on all stakeholders including dissenting creditors
- **§ 33** — Liquidation: if no plan approved within CIRP timeline; or CoC decides to liquidate; or NCLT rejects plan — liquidation order passed
- **§ 36** — Liquidation estate: all assets of corporate debtor vest in liquidation estate; not available to secured creditors who have relinquished security
- **§ 43-51** — Avoidance transactions: preferential transactions (§ 43), undervalue transactions (§ 45), extortionate credit transactions (§ 50), fraudulent trading (§ 49) — RP can apply to NCLT to set aside
- **§ 53** — Priority of claims in liquidation: insolvency costs → secured creditors (who relinquish security) → workmen's dues (24 months) → other employee dues → Central/State Government dues → remaining financial creditors → remaining debts → shareholders
- **§ 61** — CIRP timeline: 180 days from insolvency commencement date + 90 days extension (maximum 330 days including court time)
- **§ 74** — Penalties: imprisonment 3-5 years for concealment of property, false representation, obtaining credit by fraud
- **§ 95-187** — Individual insolvency: application by creditor or debtor; repayment plan process; bankruptcy order; fresh start process for low-income individuals

## Key Provisions

**CIRP timeline:**

| Stage | Deadline |
|-------|----------|
| NCLT admission/rejection | 14 days of application |
| IRP appointment | 14 days of insolvency commencement |
| Information memorandum | 30 days of IRP appointment |
| Submission of resolution plans | As set by RP (minimum 30 days, maximum 90 days from publication of information memorandum) |
| CoC approval | Within CIRP period |
| NCLT approval of plan | 30 days of CoC approval |
| Total CIRP (hard cap) | 330 days (180 + 90 + court time) |

**Moratorium (§ 13) — what stays:**
- All suits and proceedings against corporate debtor
- Execution of judgments/decrees
- Transfer/encumbrance of assets
- Supply of essential goods and services continues (§ 14(2))

**Committee of Creditors — composition:**
- Financial creditors only (banks, debenture holders, bond holders)
- Operational creditors (suppliers, employees) excluded from voting
- If no financial creditors: operational creditors form CoC
- Homebuyers (real estate projects) treated as financial creditors (2018 amendment — IBC § 5(8)(f))

**Waterfall in liquidation (§ 53):**
1. CIRP and liquidation costs
2. Workmen's dues (preceding 24 months) + secured creditors who relinquished security (pari passu)
3. Employee wages (preceding 12 months, excluding workmen)
4. Financial debts to unsecured creditors
5. Central Government / State Government dues
6. Remaining debts and dues
7. Preference shareholders
8. Equity shareholders

**Section 29A — eligibility restrictions:** Promoters of company with NPA for ≥ 1 year (wilful defaulters, those convicted of offences) cannot submit resolution plans. Prevents promoters from reacquiring companies at discounted prices.

**Key cases:**
- **Essar Steel v Standard Chartered (2019 SC):** CoC has commercial wisdom to differentiate between classes of creditors; NCLT/NCLAT cannot interfere with commercial decisions
- **Swiss Ribbons v Union of India (2019 SC):** IBC constitutional; distinction between financial and operational creditors upheld as having rational basis
- **Videocon (2021):** Consolidation of group company insolvencies permissible in exceptional circumstances
