## Definition

Constitutional amendments are formal changes to the text or interpretation of the Indian Constitution, governed by Article 368. India has a tiered amendment procedure — some provisions require simple parliamentary majority, others a special two-thirds majority, and a few additionally require ratification by half the State Legislatures.

## Current Law

**Constitution of India — Article 368**

- **Article 368(1)** — Parliament has constituent power to amend the Constitution
- **Article 368(2)** — Special majority required: two-thirds of members present and voting in each House, AND more than half of total membership of each House
- **Article 368(2) proviso** — Certain provisions additionally require ratification by legislatures of not less than one-half of the States before Presidential assent

## Key Provisions

**Three tiers of amendment:**

| Tier | Procedure | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Simple majority (ordinary legislation) | Majority of members present and voting | Article 2, 3 (new States), Second/Fifth/Sixth Schedule |
| Special majority (Article 368) | 2/3 present-and-voting + >1/2 total membership in both Houses | Most provisions — FRs, DPSPs, Parliament structure |
| Special majority + State ratification | As above + ≥50% of State Legislatures | Federal provisions — Article 54, 55, 73, 162, Chapter IV of Part V, Chapter V of Part VI, Seventh Schedule, representation of States in Parliament, Article 368 itself |

**Basic Structure Doctrine:**
- Parliament cannot amend the Constitution so as to destroy its "basic structure"
- Established in **Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973)** — 13-judge bench, 7:6 majority
- Elements of basic structure (illustrative, not exhaustive): supremacy of Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, separation of powers, federalism, secularism, judicial review, rule of law, fundamental rights, free and fair elections, independence of judiciary

**Procedure (Article 368):**
1. Bill introduced in either House of Parliament (not State Legislatures)
2. Passed in each House by special majority
3. If required, ratified by State Legislatures (simple majority sufficient in States)
4. Presidential assent — President has no power to withhold or return an amendment bill (unlike ordinary bills)

## Important Amendments

| Amendment | Year | Key Change |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| 1st | 1951 | Added Ninth Schedule; restricted FR to property |
| 7th | 1956 | States Reorganisation — linguistic states |
| 24th | 1971 | Parliament can amend FRs (overruling Golak Nath) |
| 25th | 1971 | Article 31C — DPSP laws protected from FR challenge |
| 42nd | 1976 | "Mini-Constitution" — added Fundamental Duties, Preamble words (socialist, secular, integrity), extended emergency provisions |
| 44th | 1978 | Restored several provisions reversed by 42nd Amendment |
| 52nd | 1985 | Anti-defection law (Tenth Schedule) |
| 73rd/74th | 1992 | Constitutional status to Panchayats and Municipalities |
| 86th | 2002 | Article 21A — Right to Education as FR |
| 101st | 2016 | Goods and Services Tax (GST) |
| 103rd | 2019 | 10% EWS reservation in education and employment |
| 105th | 2021 | Restored State/UT power to identify OBC sub-categories |

## Limitations on Amendment Power

- **Basic structure** cannot be amended (Kesavananda Bharati, 1973)
- **Ninth Schedule laws** challenged on FR grounds if added after 24 April 1973 are justiciable (I.R. Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu, 2007)
- Amendment bills cannot be introduced in State Legislatures
