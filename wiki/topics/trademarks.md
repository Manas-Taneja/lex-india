# Trademarks

## Definition
A trademark is a sign capable of distinguishing the goods or services of one undertaking from those of others. In India, trademark law is governed by the Trade Marks Act 1999 (replacing the Trade and Merchandise Marks Act 1958), which aligns with TRIPS obligations and enables international filing via the Madrid Protocol.

## Current Law

### Trade Marks Act 1999

- § 2(1)(zb) — "trade mark": a mark capable of being represented graphically and distinguishing the goods or services of one person from those of others; includes shape of goods, packaging, and combination of colours
- § 9 — absolute grounds for refusal: marks devoid of distinctive character; marks that are exclusively descriptive; generic marks; marks deceptive or likely to cause confusion; marks contrary to public policy or morality; marks that are functional shapes; marks containing protected emblems (Prevention of Improper Use Act 1950)
- § 11 — relative grounds for refusal: likelihood of confusion with an earlier mark; likelihood of association; marks identical/similar to well-known marks even for dissimilar goods
- § 11(6) — well-known trade marks: Registrar must consider mark well-known if it is known to a substantial segment of the relevant public; Rule 124 prescribes application procedure for recognition
- § 29 — infringement: use of identical/similar mark in course of trade for identical/similar goods without consent; use likely to cause confusion; use as part of trade name; use in advertising that takes unfair advantage
- § 30 — limits on effect of registered trade mark: honest concurrent use; use of person's own name in good faith; use of descriptive terms in good faith; prior use
- § 31 — registration as prima facie evidence of validity of mark and proprietor's right to exclusive use
- § 47 — removal for non-use: any person may apply after 5 years and 3 months from date of registration if mark not used; burden on proprietor to show genuine use
- § 56 — assignment of trade mark: may be assigned with or without goodwill; restrictions on assignments causing deception
- § 57 — rectification of register: application to Registrar or High Court to expunge or vary entry obtained by fraud or where mark has ceased to be distinctive
- § 103 — criminal penalty for falsifying or falsely applying a trade mark: minimum 6 months imprisonment (up to 3 years) and minimum fine of ₹50,000 (up to ₹2 lakh)
- § 134 — passing off suits: suit for passing off in relation to use of trade mark may be filed in District Court; passing off protects unregistered marks through common law

### Geographical Indications

- GI Act 1999: protects names/signs indicating goods originating in a specific territory where quality, reputation, or characteristic is essentially attributable to origin
- Notable GIs: Darjeeling Tea, Basmati Rice, Kanchipuram Silk, Mysore Sandal Soap, Alphonso Mango, Tirupathi Laddu

## Key Provisions

### Absolute vs Relative Grounds for Refusal

| Ground | Type | Section |
|---|---|---|
| Lacks distinctive character | Absolute | § 9(1)(a) |
| Exclusively descriptive | Absolute | § 9(1)(b) |
| Customary in trade (generic) | Absolute | § 9(1)(c) |
| Deceptive / causes confusion | Absolute | § 9(2)(a) |
| Contrary to public order / morality | Absolute | § 9(2)(b) |
| Identical/similar to earlier mark — confusion likely | Relative | § 11(1) |
| Identical/similar to well-known mark | Relative | § 11(2) |

### Madrid Protocol — International Filing

- India joined the Madrid Protocol in 2013
- An Indian applicant can file a single international application through the Indian Trade Marks Registry (office of origin) designating multiple member countries
- WIPO administers the International Register; national offices conduct examination under their own law

### Passing Off (§ 134 + Common Law)

Elements the plaintiff must establish:

1. Goodwill and reputation in the mark
2. Misrepresentation by defendant likely to deceive or confuse
3. Actual damage or likelihood of damage to goodwill

Passing off protects unregistered marks; registration confers additional statutory remedies under § 29.
