# Maintenance and Alimony

## Definition
Maintenance is the legal obligation of one person to provide financial support to another who is unable to support themselves. It covers spousal support (alimony), child support, and parental support. India's maintenance law operates across personal law statutes and a secular criminal provision.

## Current Law

### Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act 1956 (HAMA)
- § 18 — A Hindu wife is entitled to be maintained by her husband during her lifetime; grounds for separate residence without losing right include cruelty, leprosy, another wife living, keeping concubine, conversion, or any other reasonable cause
- § 19 — Widowed daughter-in-law entitled to maintenance from estate of father-in-law if she cannot be maintained from her own estate or her husband's estate
- § 20 — Obligation to maintain: Hindu is bound to maintain legitimate / illegitimate children and aged / infirm parents
- § 23 — Quantum of maintenance: court considers status of parties, claimant's own income/property, income of person liable, number of persons to be maintained, claimant's reasonable wants

### Code of Criminal Procedure 1973 § 125 / BNSS 2023 § 144
- Secular provision applicable to persons of all religions
- Wife (including divorced wife who has not remarried), legitimate/illegitimate children (below 18 or beyond if suffering from physical/mental disability), and parents who are unable to maintain themselves may apply
- No statutory cap on amount; magistrate orders what appears "just"
- Interim maintenance and expenses of proceedings also available
- SC in Rajnesh v Neha (2020) issued comprehensive guidelines: (i) maintenance under different statutes must be adjusted to avoid double benefit; (ii) wife must disclose assets and income; (iii) interim maintenance interim arrangement within 60 days; (iv) modification possible on change in circumstances
- BNSS § 144 substantially re-enacts CrPC § 125 with minor procedural changes

### Hindu Marriage Act 1955
- § 24 — Pendente lite maintenance and expenses: either spouse may seek maintenance during pendency of HMA proceedings; court considers income and expenses
- § 25 — Permanent alimony and maintenance: at time of decree or later, court may order gross sum or monthly/periodical payment; modifiable on change in circumstances; ceases on remarriage of recipient

### Special Marriage Act 1954
- § 36 — Pendente lite maintenance (mirrors HMA § 24)
- § 37 — Permanent alimony (mirrors HMA § 25)

### Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act 1986 and Case Law
- Enacted after Shah Bano (1985) to ostensibly limit Muslim divorced women's maintenance to iddat period
- Danial Latifi v Union of India (SC 2001): SC read the Act broadly — husband's obligation is to make "reasonable and fair provision" for the entire future of the divorced wife, not merely during iddat; lump-sum payment upfront for future must be sufficient
- § 3 — Entitlements: reasonable and fair provision and maintenance for iddat period; mahr; return of dowry; maintenance for children up to 2 years

### Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019
- Enacted post Shayara Bano (SC 2017) striking down instantaneous triple talaq
- § 3 — Instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) void and illegal
- § 4 — Husband who pronounces triple talaq: imprisonment up to 3 years + fine (cognisable, non-bailable)
- § 5 — Divorced woman entitled to maintenance for herself and dependent children from husband (magistrate to determine amount)

### Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act 2007
- § 4 — Children / relatives obliged to maintain parents / senior citizens who are unable to maintain themselves
- § 9 — Maintenance Tribunal (SDM or equivalent) may order up to ₹10,000 per month (states may enhance; e.g., Himachal Pradesh enhanced to ₹20,000); order enforceable as arrears of land revenue
- § 24 — Abandonment of senior citizen is punishable with imprisonment up to 3 months or fine up to ₹5,000 or both

## Key Provisions

### Comparison of Maintenance Forums
| Forum | Statute | Who Can Apply | Ceiling |
|-------|---------|---------------|---------|
| Family Court / District Court | HMA §§ 24-25; HAMA §§ 18-23 | Spouses; children; parents (Hindu) | None (discretionary) |
| Magistrate (Judicial / Executive) | CrPC § 125 / BNSS § 144 | Wife; children; parents (all religions) | None |
| Maintenance Tribunal | Senior Citizens Act 2007 | Parents / senior citizens | ₹10,000/month (state may increase) |
| Family Court (Muslim divorced wife) | Muslim Women Act 1986 | Divorced Muslim wife | Reasonable provision (Danial Latifi) |

### Factors for Quantum of Maintenance
| Factor | Relevance |
|--------|-----------|
| Income and property of both parties | Primary determinant |
| Standard of living during marriage | Baseline for quantum |
| Number of dependants | Shared burden |
| Health and age of claimant | Need assessment |
| Claimant's own earning capacity | Deducted from need |
| Conduct of parties | Discretionary weight |
