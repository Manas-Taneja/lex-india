## Definition

Drug offences in India are primarily governed by the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act), which prohibits manufacture, possession, sale, purchase, transport, warehousing, import/export, and use of narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances. The Act creates a graduated penalty structure based on the quantity involved.

## Current Law

**Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (NDPS Act)**

- **§ 2** — Definitions: narcotic drugs, psychotropic substances, small quantity, commercial quantity
- **§ 8** — Prohibition: no person shall produce, manufacture, possess, sell, purchase, transport, import inter-State, export inter-State, import into India, export from India, or use any narcotic drug or psychotropic substance except for medical/scientific purposes under a licence
- **§ 20** — Cannabis offences: up to 1 year (small quantity), 10 years (between small and commercial), rigorous imprisonment 10–20 years + fine ₹1–2 lakh (commercial quantity)
- **§ 21** — Manufactured drug offences: same tiered structure
- **§ 22** — Psychotropic substances offences: same tiered structure
- **§ 27** — Consumption of narcotic drug or psychotropic substance: up to 1 year, or fine, or both (for small quantity); 6 months–1 year for cannabis
- **§ 27A** — Financing illicit traffic and harbouring offenders: rigorous imprisonment 10–20 years + fine ₹1–2 lakh
- **§ 29** — Abetment and criminal conspiracy: same punishment as the substantive offence
- **§ 37** — Bail restrictions: for commercial-quantity offences, bail shall not be granted unless the court is satisfied that there are reasonable grounds for believing the accused is not guilty and will not commit any offence while on bail (reverse burden)
- **§ 50** — Conditions under which search of a person shall be conducted: must be before a Gazetted Officer or Magistrate if the person demands
- **§ 54** — Presumption of culpable mental state: raises presumption of guilt once possession is proved; accused must rebut

## Key Provisions

**Three-tier punishment based on quantity:**

| Quantity | Punishment |
|----------|-----------|
| Small quantity | Up to 1 year, or fine up to ₹10,000, or both |
| More than small, less than commercial | Up to 10 years + fine up to ₹1 lakh |
| Commercial quantity | Rigorous imprisonment 10–20 years + fine ₹1–2 lakh |

*Quantities are drug-specific and notified by the Central Government. Example: heroin — small = 5g, commercial = 250g; cannabis — small = 1kg, commercial = 20kg.*

**Section 37 — Reverse bail burden:** NDPS is one of the few laws where the accused must satisfy the court they deserve bail for commercial-quantity offences. This is a departure from the ordinary rule that prosecution proves guilt.

**Section 50 — Search procedure:** Mandatory compliance; non-compliance vitiates the search and can lead to acquittal even if drugs are found. The accused must be informed of the right to be searched before a Magistrate or Gazetted Officer.

**Section 54 — Presumption:** Once possession of a notified drug is proved, the burden shifts to the accused to prove absence of culpable mental state.

**Addicts and treatment:** § 64A — An addict who voluntarily undergoes treatment at a recognised centre may be immune from prosecution.

**Death penalty:** The NDPS Act itself does not prescribe death penalty. However, for a repeat offence for commercial quantity, courts may impose death in exceptional circumstances (State-level amendment applicable in some States, e.g., Maharashtra under § 31A after 1989 amendment — rigorous imprisonment for life or death for repeat commercial-quantity offenders).
