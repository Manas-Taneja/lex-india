## Definition

Domestic violence under Indian law encompasses physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, and economic abuse inflicted on a woman by her husband, male partner, or male relatives in a shared household. The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (DV Act) is a civil remedies statute — it provides protection orders, residence orders, and maintenance, primarily through the Magistrate's court, without requiring a criminal conviction.

## Current Law

**Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (DV Act)**

- **§ 2(a)** — Aggrieved person: any woman in a domestic relationship who alleges domestic violence
- **§ 2(f)** — Domestic relationship: relationship between two persons who live or have lived together in a shared household, connected by blood, marriage, adoption, or as live-in partners
- **§ 2(g)** — Domestic violence: includes physical, sexual, verbal/emotional, and economic abuse (as defined in § 3)
- **§ 3** — Definition of domestic violence: acts/omissions/commissions causing bodily pain, harm, danger to life; sexual abuse; insults, ridicule, humiliation; harassment to meet unlawful dowry demand; threats; economic deprivation (not providing money for maintenance or necessities)
- **§ 12** — Application to Magistrate by aggrieved person, Protection Officer, or any person acting on her behalf
- **§ 17** — Right to reside in shared household — even if the woman has no title or ownership
- **§ 18** — Protection orders: prohibit the respondent from committing violence, entering the aggrieved person's place of work/school, communicating with her
- **§ 19** — Residence orders: prevent respondent from dispossessing the aggrieved person; direct respondent to secure alternative accommodation
- **§ 20** — Monetary relief: compensation for loss of earnings, medical expenses, maintenance for child
- **§ 21** — Custody orders: temporary custody of child
- **§ 22** — Compensation orders: damages for injuries including mental torture
- **§ 23** — Interim and ex-parte orders (emergency relief without hearing the respondent)
- **§ 31** — Breach of protection order: cognizable, non-bailable offence — imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine up to ₹20,000, or both
- **§ 32** — Cognizance of offence: only by Magistrate (not Sessions Court)

**BNS, 2023:**
- **§ 84** — Cruelty by husband or his relatives: imprisonment up to 3 years + fine (criminal prosecution, distinct from DV Act civil remedies)
- **§ 85** — Dowry death (death of woman within 7 years of marriage due to harassment over dowry): imprisonment for life or 7+ years

## Key Provisions

**Civil not criminal:** The DV Act itself is civil in nature — breach of protection order is criminal. Police can act under the DV Act without the woman filing an FIR under BNS.

**Live-in relationships covered:** "Domestic relationship" includes women in live-in relationships — the Supreme Court confirmed this in D. Velusamy v D. Patchaiammal (2010).

**Shared household (§ 2(s)):** The woman's right to reside in the shared household cannot be defeated merely because the property belongs to in-laws — she has a right to remain until proper alternative accommodation is arranged.

**Protection Officer:** Each district has Protection Officers appointed by the State Government. They assist aggrieved persons, prepare domestic incident reports, and ensure enforcement of orders.

**Economic abuse (§ 3(iv)):** Withholding maintenance, preventing employment, taking away income, forcing out of home — all constitute domestic violence. This is broader than the maintenance provisions under personal laws.

**Dowry offences (separate track):**
- **Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 (§ 3):** Giving or taking dowry — imprisonment 5+ years + fine ₹15,000 or value of dowry (whichever higher)
- **BNS § 84:** Cruelty including harassment for dowry — 3 years (used alongside DV Act for criminal prosecution)
- **BNS § 85:** Dowry death within 7 years — life imprisonment
