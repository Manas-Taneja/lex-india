## Definition

Emergency provisions in Part XVIII of the Indian Constitution (Articles 352–360) allow the Central Government to assume extraordinary powers during threats to national security, constitutional breakdown in a State, or financial instability. During an emergency, normal federal and rights-based arrangements are suspended or altered.

## Current Law

**Constitution of India, Part XVIII — Articles 352–360**

Three distinct types of emergency:

### 1. National Emergency (Article 352)

- **Grounds:** War, external aggression, or **armed rebellion** (word changed from "internal disturbance" by 44th Amendment, 1978)
- **Proclamation by:** President, on written advice of Cabinet
- **Parliamentary approval:** Both Houses must approve within one month by special majority (2/3 present-and-voting + >1/2 total membership)
- **Duration:** Six months at a time; renewable by further parliamentary resolutions
- **Revocation:** Lok Sabha can pass a simple-majority resolution disapproving; President must revoke

**Effects of National Emergency:**
- Article 353: Parliament can legislate on State List subjects; Centre can give executive directions to States
- Article 354: Provisions for distribution of revenues between Union and States can be modified
- Article 358: Article 19 (six freedoms) suspended automatically for duration
- Article 359: President may by order suspend enforcement of any other FRs (except Articles 20 and 21 — **cannot be suspended even during National Emergency** per 44th Amendment)
- Lok Sabha term can be extended one year at a time (Article 83)

**Declared:** 1962 (China war), 1971 (Pakistan war), 1975–1977 (internal disturbance — the most controversial, overturned by 44th Amendment's stricter safeguards)

### 2. President's Rule / State Emergency (Article 356)

- **Grounds:** Constitutional machinery in a State has failed (Governor's report or President's own satisfaction)
- **Parliamentary approval:** Both Houses must approve within two months by simple majority
- **Duration:** Six months at a time; maximum three years (beyond one year requires National Emergency to be in force OR Election Commission to certify elections cannot be held)
- **Effects:** President assumes all functions of State Government; Parliament legislates for the State; State Legislature suspended or dissolved

**Safeguards (post-S.R. Bommai v Union of India, 1994):**
- Floor test mandatory before recommending President's Rule
- Court can examine if the proclamation has any rational basis
- If proclamation is invalid, Court can restore dissolved State Legislature

### 3. Financial Emergency (Article 360)

- **Grounds:** Financial stability or credit of India or any part thereof threatened
- **Parliamentary approval:** Both Houses must approve within two months by simple majority
- **Effects:** President can direct reduction of salaries of all government servants including judges; financial bills of States require Presidential assent
- **Never declared** in India's history

## Key Provisions & Safeguards

| Safeguard | Article | Detail |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Written Cabinet advice | 352 | President cannot proclaim on oral advice |
| Special majority approval | 352 | Prevents simple-majority abuse |
| Lok Sabha revocation | 352(2B) | Lok Sabha can revoke by simple majority |
| Articles 20 & 21 inviolable | 359 | Right to life and protection from double jeopardy cannot be suspended |
| Article 19 auto-suspension | 358 | Only during war/external aggression, not armed rebellion |
| Judicial review of 356 | Bommai | Courts can examine whether factual basis exists |

## Key Cases

- **A.K. Gopalan v State of Madras (1950)** — Early interpretation of Article 21 during preventive detention
- **S.R. Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — Landmark: 9-judge bench; floor test required; secularism is basic structure; courts can review 356 proclamation
- **Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — 42nd Amendment's attempt to exclude judicial review of emergency struck down
