## Definition

Competition law in India is governed by the Competition Act, 2002 (as amended 2023), administered by the Competition Commission of India (CCI). The Act prohibits anti-competitive agreements (§ 3), abuse of dominant position (§ 4), and regulates combinations (mergers and acquisitions) that may appreciably affect competition (§ 5-6). The Competition (Amendment) Act, 2023 introduced deal-value thresholds for merger filings, a settlement and commitment mechanism, and enhanced penalty provisions.

## Current Law

**Competition Act, 2002 (as amended by Competition Amendment Act, 2023)**

- **§ 2(h)** — Enterprise: person or department of government engaged in any activity relating to production, supply, distribution, acquisition, or control of goods or services
- **§ 2(r)** — Relevant market: relevant product market + relevant geographic market (defined by substitutability from consumer and supplier perspective)
- **§ 3** — Anti-competitive agreements: agreements between enterprises/persons at horizontal level (cartel, price-fixing, output restriction, market allocation, bid-rigging) are presumed to have AAEC; vertical agreements (exclusive supply, exclusive distribution, resale price maintenance, refusal to deal, tie-in) assessed under rule of reason
- **§ 3(3)** — Horizontal agreements: price-fixing, limiting production, allocation of markets/customers — void per se (presumed AAEC without requiring proof)
- **§ 3(4)** — Vertical agreements: only prohibited if causing AAEC; rule of reason applies
- **§ 3(5)** — Exemptions: rights under IP laws; export agreements not affecting domestic competition
- **§ 4** — Abuse of dominant position: dominant enterprise prohibited from imposing unfair/discriminatory prices, limiting supply, technical/scientific development, denying market access, using dominant position in one market to enter/protect another; predatory pricing
- **§ 4(2)** — Dominant position: position of strength in relevant market allowing enterprise to operate independently of competitive forces
- **§ 5** — Combinations threshold: merger/acquisition requiring prior CCI approval — assets > ₹2,000 crore (India) or turnover > ₹6,000 crore (India); or worldwide assets > USD 1 billion with India assets > ₹1,000 crore (2023 amendment increased thresholds)
- **§ 5A (2023)** — Deal value threshold: combination with deal value > ₹2,000 crore where acquired entity has substantial business operations in India — prior approval required even if asset/turnover thresholds not met (targets digital markets)
- **§ 6** — Combinations void if causing AAEC: parties must notify CCI; no consummation before approval
- **§ 6(2A)** — Review period: CCI must pass order within 150 calendar days (increased from 210 working days by 2023 amendment); if no order — deemed approved
- **§ 19** — Inquiry by CCI: can inquire into AAEC on its own motion, on complaint by any person, or on reference from Central/State Government or statutory authority
- **§ 26** — Investigation: DG (Director General) investigates on CCI direction; report submitted to CCI
- **§ 27** — Orders after finding of AAEC: CCI may pass cease and desist order; impose penalty up to 10% of average turnover (3 years); division of enterprise in § 4 violations
- **§ 31** — Combinations order: approve; approve with modification; reject (if AAEC found and cannot be remedied)
- **§ 42A (2023)** — Settlement: parties may propose settlement before final order; CCI may accept; reduces penalty
- **§ 42B (2023)** — Commitment: during investigation, parties may offer commitments to address competition concerns
- **§ 46** — Leniency: first member of cartel to disclose full information — 100% reduction in penalty; subsequent disclosers — 50% or 30% reduction

## Key Provisions

**AAEC factors (§ 19(3)):**
- Market share; barriers to entry; level of combination
- Countervailing buying power; likelihood of foreclosure
- Extent of effective competition; benefits to consumers
- Economic efficiencies (defence in combinations)

**Dominance ≠ violation:** Holding dominant position is not illegal. Only abuse of dominance violates § 4. Market share above 50% is strong indicator of dominance but not conclusive.

**Horizontal vs vertical agreements:**

| Agreement type | Test | If AAEC found |
|---------------|------|---------------|
| Horizontal (§ 3(3)) | Presumed AAEC (per se) | Void + penalty |
| Vertical (§ 3(4)) | Rule of reason | Void + penalty |
| IP licensing (§ 3(5)) | Exempt from § 3 | N/A |

**Combination review — thresholds (post-2023 amendment):**

| Threshold | Trigger |
|-----------|---------|
| India assets > ₹2,000 crore OR India turnover > ₹6,000 crore | Mandatory notification |
| Worldwide assets > USD 1 billion + India assets > ₹1,000 crore | Mandatory notification |
| Deal value > ₹2,000 crore + substantial India operations | Mandatory notification (new) |

**Penalties:**
- Anti-competitive agreements/abuse: up to 10% of average turnover (preceding 3 years)
- Cartel: up to 10% of turnover OR 3× profit for each year of continuation (whichever is higher)
- Failure to notify combination: up to 1% of turnover/assets
- Non-compliance with CCI order: ₹1 lakh–₹10 lakh per day; continued non-compliance → criminal prosecution

**CCI v SEBI jurisdiction:** In matters involving both securities regulation and competition, CCI and SEBI (or other sector regulators) may have overlapping jurisdiction. CCI consults sector regulator under § 21; final order after consultation.

**Key cases:**
- **CCI v Coordination Committee of Artists, Kerala (2017 SC):** Film exhibitors' collective decision to boycott a production house = anti-competitive horizontal agreement
- **Google (CCI, 2022):** Abuse of dominant position in Android mobile device market; ₹1,337 crore penalty
- **COMPAT → NCLAT:** Competition Appellate Tribunal replaced by NCLAT (National Company Law Appellate Tribunal) under 2017 Finance Act
