## Definition

A First Information Report (FIR) is the written document prepared by the police when they receive information about the commission of a cognizable offence. It sets the criminal process in motion — police can investigate and arrest without a magistrate's order for cognizable offences. The FIR is not a charge sheet; it is the first step.

## Current Law

**Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)**

- **§ 173** — Information in cognizable cases, FIR registration (mandatory)
- **§ 174** — Police officer's powers to investigate cognizable case
- **§ 175** — No FIR: police can still investigate non-cognizable offences with magistrate's order
- **§ 176** — Investigation procedure
- **§ 173(1) proviso** — Electronic FIR: FIR can now be filed online or via electronic communication (new in BNSS)

## Key Provisions

**Mandatory registration (§ 173):** Police must register an FIR for every cognizable offence reported to them. Refusal to register is an offence. The Supreme Court (Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of UP, 2014) held that registration is mandatory, not discretionary.

**Cognizable vs non-cognizable:**
- *Cognizable offence*: Police can arrest without warrant and investigate without magistrate's order (murder, theft, rape, dacoity, etc.)
- *Non-cognizable offence*: Police need magistrate's order to investigate (assault without grievous hurt, cheating below threshold, etc.)

**Zero FIR:** An FIR can be filed at any police station regardless of jurisdiction. The receiving station must register it and transfer it to the jurisdictionally correct station. Introduced by Supreme Court; now codified in BNSS.

**Electronic FIR (§ 173 proviso — new in BNSS):** Information given by electronic communication shall also be recorded as FIR. The informant must sign it within 3 days of such communication. This enables online FIR filing.

**Copy to informant:** The informant is entitled to a free copy of the FIR. Refusal is actionable.

**Cancellation/Closure:** If on investigation the police find no offence, they file a closure/cancellation report before the magistrate. The complainant can protest before the magistrate.

**Anticipatory vs post-arrest:** FIR precedes arrest in cognizable cases (police can arrest based on FIR). In non-cognizable cases, no arrest without magistrate's warrant.

## Transition Note

Previously governed by **Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC)**:
- CrPC § 154 → BNSS § 173 (FIR registration)
- CrPC § 155 → BNSS § 175 (non-cognizable cases)
- CrPC § 156 → BNSS § 174 (investigation powers)

Key changes in BNSS:
1. Electronic/online FIR explicitly recognised (§ 173 proviso)
2. Zero FIR codified in the statute
3. Time limits for investigation introduced (§ 173(1): within 14 days for minor offences)
