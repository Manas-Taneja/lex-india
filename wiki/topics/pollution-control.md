# Pollution Control

## Definition
India's pollution control framework rests on three pillar statutes — the Water Act 1974, the Air Act 1981, and the Environment (Protection) Act 1986 — enforced by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs), supplemented by the NGT's supervisory jurisdiction.

## Current Law

### Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act 1974
- § 16 — Establishes Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB); § 17 functions of CPCB including setting effluent standards and coordinating with SPCBs
- § 18 — State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) constituted in each state
- § 24 — Prohibition on knowingly causing or permitting the poisoning, polluting, or fouling of any stream or well
- § 25 — Consent to establish (CTE) required from SPCB before establishing any industry, operation, or process likely to discharge sewage/trade effluent
- § 26 — Consent to operate (CTO) required before operating any such industry; SPCB may impose conditions including effluent standards

### Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act 1981
- § 21 — No person shall establish or operate any industrial plant in an air pollution control area without prior consent of SPCB
- § 22 — Prohibition on emission of air pollutants in excess of SPCB-prescribed standards
- CPCB publishes National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS); PM2.5, PM10, NO2, SO2, CO, ozone among regulated pollutants
- Vehicular pollution: Auto Fuel Policy prescribes Bharat Stage (BS) emission norms; BS-VI mandatory from April 2020

### Environment (Protection) Act 1986 (rules and notifications)
- Noise Pollution (Regulation and Control) Rules 2000: day/night ambient noise limits by zone (industrial, commercial, residential, silence zones); prohibits loudspeakers beyond prescribed decibels
- Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules 2016: classifies hazardous waste; requires authorisation from SPCB; manifest system for tracking
- Biomedical Waste Management Rules 2016: colour-coded segregation (yellow, red, white, blue bags); mandatory for hospitals and health care facilities
- Plastic Waste Management Rules 2016 (amended 2022): ban on identified single-use plastic items (< 75 micron) from 1 July 2022; EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) mandatory for producers/importers/brand owners
- E-Waste (Management) Rules 2022: EPR obligation on producers; take-back schemes; targets for collection and channelisation

### NGT Enforcement
- NGT Act 2010 § 14: original jurisdiction over substantial questions relating to environment
- § 15: relief and compensation to victims of pollution; restoration of damaged environment
- § 20: penalty up to ₹10 crore for non-compliance with NGT orders

## Key Provisions

### Consent Mechanism (Water Act + Air Act)
| Consent Type | Trigger | Issuing Authority | Key Condition |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| Consent to Establish (CTE) | Before construction/setup | SPCB | Site approval, pollution control plan |
| Consent to Operate (CTO) | Before commencing operations | SPCB | Effluent/emission within prescribed limits |
| Renewal of CTO | Periodic (1–5 years) | SPCB | Compliance verification, updated monitoring data |

### Waste Management Rules — EPR Framework
| Waste Type | Governing Rules | EPR Obligation |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Plastic packaging | Plastic Waste Rules 2016/2022 | Producers must collect and recycle equivalent of their market supply |
| E-waste | E-Waste Rules 2022 | Collection targets rising annually; PRO or self-fulfil |
| Batteries | Battery Waste Management Rules 2022 | Take-back and recycling |
| Tyres | Hazardous Waste Rules 2016 | Collect and channelise to authorised recyclers |

### Landmark Cases
| Case | Forum | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| MC Mehta v. Union of India (Ganga Pollution), 1988 | Supreme Court | Directed closure of tanneries; established polluter-pays principle in India |
| MC Mehta v. Union of India (Vehicular Pollution), 1998 | Supreme Court | Ordered phase-out of leaded petrol; CNG conversion of Delhi fleet |
| Sterlite Industries v. Union of India, 2013 | Supreme Court | Upheld temporary closure; affirmed SPCB's power to close units in public interest |
| Vellore Citizens Welfare Forum v. Union of India, 1996 | Supreme Court | Established precautionary principle and polluter-pays principle as part of Indian environmental law |

### GRAP (Graded Response Action Plan) — Delhi NCR
| Stage | AQI Range | Key Restrictions |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Stage I (Poor) | 201–300 | Mechanised sweeping; dust control measures |
| Stage II (Very Poor) | 301–400 | Diesel generator sets restricted; C&D waste controls |
| Stage III (Severe) | 401–450 | BS-III petrol and BS-IV diesel 4-wheelers banned in NCR |
| Stage IV (Severe+) | >450 | Trucks banned from entering Delhi; schools/colleges may close |
