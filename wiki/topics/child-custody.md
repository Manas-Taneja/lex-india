# Child Custody

## Definition
Child custody determines who has the legal right and responsibility to care for a minor child. Indian law distinguishes between guardianship (legal authority over person and property) and custody (physical residence and day-to-day care). The welfare of the child is the paramount consideration overriding all other claims.

## Current Law

### Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act 1956 (HMGA)
- § 6 — Natural guardians of a Hindu minor:
  - Father for a legitimate boy and an unmarried girl
  - Mother for a legitimate child below 5 years
  - Mother for an illegitimate boy and an illegitimate unmarried girl
  - Husband for a married girl
- § 7 — Natural guardian of adopted child: adoptive father, then adoptive mother
- § 8 — Powers of natural guardian: act in child's best interests; court permission needed to sell / mortgage immovable property
- § 13 — Welfare of minor is paramount: no person's right as natural guardian shall be enforceable if contrary to the welfare of the minor (explicitly overrides § 6 hierarchy)

### Guardians and Wards Act 1890 (GWA)
- § 7 — District Court may, on application, appoint or declare a guardian of the person or property of a minor if it considers this necessary for the welfare of the minor
- § 17 — Welfare of the minor is the paramount consideration; court shall consider age, sex, religion of minor; character and capacity of proposed guardian; wishes of deceased parents; any existing custody arrangement
- § 19 — Court shall not appoint guardian if child's father is alive and is fit to be guardian (subject to welfare test)
- § 25 — Custody order enforceable; court can issue warrant for return of minor
- §§ 7-26 collectively govern appointment, powers, and removal of guardians

### Githa Hariharan v Reserve Bank of India (SC 1999)
The Supreme Court read the word "after" in HMGA § 6(a) (natural guardian is father, and after him the mother) to mean "in the absence of" rather than "after the death of." A mother can therefore be the natural guardian during the father's lifetime if the father is absent or otherwise unable to act, preventing discrimination on the basis of sex.

### Welfare Test — Factors Applied by Courts
Courts in India apply a multi-factor welfare test drawing on GWA § 17 and constitutional principles:

| Factor | Judicial Treatment |
|--------|-------------------|
| Age and sex of child | Tender-years doctrine: young children typically with mother |
| Continuity of care | Established caregiver relationship preferred |
| Child's wishes | Given weight proportional to maturity (no fixed age threshold) |
| Financial capacity of parent | Relevant but not determinative |
| Emotional bond | Direct assessment in chambers |
| Sibling separation | Courts reluctant to split siblings |
| Parent's conduct (cruelty, addiction) | Material disqualifier |
| Stability of home environment | Employment, residence, support system |

### International Custody
- India is not a signatory to the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction 1980
- Indian courts apply the welfare test independently; they are not bound to return the child to the country of habitual residence
- SC in Surya Vadanan v State of Tamil Nadu (2015): courts should show comity and ordinarily return child to country of habitual residence unless contrary to welfare; courts should act swiftly (within 6 weeks)
- SC in Nithya Anand Raghavan v State of NCT of Delhi (2017): welfare test is the final touchstone; comity is a factor, not a mandate

### POCSO and Custody
- Where a parent is the accused in a POCSO matter involving the child, courts may suspend that parent's custody and visitation rights during investigation and trial
- Protection Officers and Child Welfare Committees under JJ Act may make interim custody arrangements

### CrPC § 97 / BNSS Equivalent
- Magistrate may issue search warrant to recover any minor unlawfully detained
- Used in parental abduction cases as an emergency remedy

## Key Provisions

### Types of Custody Orders
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Physical custody | Child lives with custodial parent |
| Legal custody | Authority to make decisions on education, health, religion |
| Sole custody | One parent has both physical and legal custody |
| Joint custody | Both parents share physical and/or legal custody |
| Visitation / Parenting time | Non-custodial parent's structured access rights |

### Procedure Highlights
- Petition under GWA § 7 or HMA § 26 / SMA § 38 filed in Family Court or District Court
- In-camera proceedings for examination of child; child not to be cross-examined in open court
- Guardian ad litem or amicus curiae may be appointed to represent child's independent interests
- Parenting plan: courts increasingly direct parties to submit a parenting plan covering residence, education, holidays, medical decisions, and communication (Nil Ratan Kundu v Abhijit Kundu, SC 2008)
- Interim custody: available at any stage; status quo typically maintained pending final order
