## Definition

A negotiable instrument is a written document that entitles its holder to a sum of money, which is transferable by endorsement and delivery (or delivery alone if bearer), so that a bona fide transferee for value without notice of defect (holder in due course) takes free from defects in the transferor's title. In India, negotiable instruments are governed by the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 (NI Act). The three primary instruments are promissory notes, bills of exchange, and cheques. Section 138 (cheque dishonour) — a criminal provision — is the most litigated section in Indian courts.

## Current Law

**Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881**

- **§ 4** — Promissory note: unconditional promise in writing, signed by maker, to pay a certain sum to, or to the order of, a certain person, or the bearer
- **§ 5** — Bill of exchange: unconditional order in writing, signed by drawer, directing a certain person (drawee) to pay a certain sum to, or to the order of, a certain person (payee) or bearer
- **§ 6** — Cheque: bill of exchange drawn on a specified banker, payable on demand; includes truncated cheque (electronic image) and electronic cheque
- **§ 8** — Holder: person entitled in their own name to the possession and payment of the instrument
- **§ 9** — Holder in due course (HDC): person who, for consideration, became holder before maturity, without notice of defect or dishonour; HDC takes free from all defects of title of prior parties
- **§ 13** — Negotiable instrument: promissory note, bill of exchange, or cheque payable to bearer or to order
- **§ 15** — Endorsement: signing on back or face; for negotiation, for collection (restrictive), conditionally, in blank (bearer instrument thereafter)
- **§ 31** — Liability of drawer: liable to compensate holder if drawee (bank) refuses payment
- **§ 32** — Liability of maker/acceptor: primarily liable — unconditional promise to pay
- **§ 36** — Liability of endorser: on dishonour and notice, liable to all subsequent parties
- **§ 46** — Delivery essential: instrument incomplete and revocable until delivery — constructive or actual
- **§ 85** — Protection of paying banker: if cheque bears indorsement of payee (or purports to be), bank paying in good faith/due course is discharged; bank not liable even if endorsement forged (§ 85A for account-payee cheques)
- **§ 87** — Material alteration voids instrument: alteration of date, sum, time of payment, place of payment, parties' names — makes instrument void against anyone who was party before alteration unless they consent
- **§ 99** — Noting and protest: on dishonour, holder may note the fact with a notary; protest = formal certificate of dishonour (not mandatory for internal instruments)
- **§ 138** — Dishonour of cheque for insufficient funds: if cheque returned unpaid for insufficient funds or exceeds agreed limit — criminal offence; imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to twice the cheque amount or both
- **§ 139** — Presumption: court presumes cheque was issued for debt/liability unless rebutted
- **§ 141** — Liability of company: if cheque issued by company, every director/officer in charge at the time is deemed guilty (unless they prove no knowledge or exercised due diligence)
- **§ 142** — Complaint must be filed within 1 month of receiving notice of dishonour; cognisable by Magistrate First Class only; complainant must be payee or holder
- **§ 143A** — Interim compensation: Magistrate may order drawer to pay interim compensation up to 20% of cheque amount during trial
- **§ 148** — Appellate court may order deposit of minimum 20% of fine as condition of hearing appeal

## Key Provisions

**Three instruments compared:**

| Feature | Promissory Note | Bill of Exchange | Cheque |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|--------|
| Parties | Maker + Payee | Drawer + Drawee + Payee | Drawer + Bank (drawee) + Payee |
| Acceptance | Not needed | Drawee must accept | Not needed (payable on demand) |
| Notice of dishonour | Required | Required | Required for § 138 prosecution |
| Stamp duty | Required (unstamped = inadmissible) | Required | Not required |
| Validity period | As per agreement | As per agreement | 3 months from date |

**Holder in due course (§ 9) — privileges:**

1. Takes free from defects of title of prior parties
2. Every prior party liable to HDC
3. Instrument presumed to have been accepted/endorsed for consideration
4. No plea of failure of consideration available against HDC

**Section 138 — cheque dishonour prosecution — procedure:**

1. Cheque presented within 3 months of date
2. Bank returns cheque unpaid (memo of dishonour)
3. Payee sends demand notice within 30 days of dishonour
4. Drawer fails to pay within 15 days of receiving notice
5. Complaint filed within 1 month of expiry of 15-day period

**Presumptions under §§ 118-139:**
- Consideration: every negotiable instrument made/accepted/endorsed/transferred is presumed to be for consideration
- Date: instrument drawn on the stated date
- Time of acceptance: before maturity
- Time of transfer: before maturity
- Order of endorsements: in order in which they appear
- Stamp: duly stamped
- Holder is HDC (rebuttable)
- Cheque issued for debt (§ 139 — rebuttable)

**Account-payee cheques (§ 123-131A):** Crossed cheques restrict payment through banker. Account payee (special crossing) restricts collection only to payee's account. Collecting banker protected for crossed cheques collected in good faith.

**Key cases:**
- **Dashrath Rupsingh Rathod v State of Maharashtra (2014 SC):** Territorial jurisdiction lies only at place where cheque is presented (drawer's bank branch) — overruled by 2015 amendment
- **2015 Amendment (§ 142(2)):** Complaint can be filed where cheque is delivered for presentation — payee's branch; or where drawee bank is located
