## Definition

Bail is the temporary release of an accused person from custody, upon giving security that they will appear before the court when required. It is a right in bailable offences and a discretionary relief in non-bailable offences. The fundamental principle is that bail is the rule and jail is the exception for under-trial prisoners.

## Current Law

**Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)**

- **§ 478** — Definition of bail, bailable offence, non-bailable offence
- **§ 479** — Bail in bailable offences (right, not discretion)
- **§ 480** — Bail in non-bailable offences (discretion of court)
- **§ 481** — Bail for indigent accused
- **§ 482** — Bail for persons accused of non-bailable offence (police officer power)
- **§ 483** — Anticipatory bail (Sessions Court or High Court)
- **§ 484** — Special powers of High Court and Sessions Court
- **§ 479 proviso** — Under-trial bail: accused who has served half of maximum sentence for that offence shall be released on bail (key reform)

## Key Provisions

**Bailable offences (§ 479):** Bail is a right. The police officer or court must release the accused on bail if they furnish the required surety.

**Non-bailable offences (§ 480):** Bail is discretionary. Court considers:
- Nature and gravity of accusation
- Antecedents of the accused
- Possibility of fleeing justice
- Safety of the community
- Likelihood of interfering with witnesses

**Anticipatory bail (§ 483):** A person who has reason to believe they may be arrested can apply to Sessions Court or High Court for pre-arrest bail. Court can impose conditions (e.g., surrender passport, report to police, not leave India).

**Under-trial reform (§ 479 proviso — new in BNSS):** An accused who has undergone detention for half the maximum period of imprisonment for that offence shall be released on bail. First-time offenders are given priority. This addresses the massive under-trial population in Indian prisons. Exception: if charged with offence punishable by death.

**Cancellation of bail (§ 484):** Court that granted bail can cancel it if the accused misuses liberty, tampers with evidence, or absconds.

## Transition Note

Previously governed by **Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC)**:
- CrPC § 436 → BNSS § 479 (bailable offences)
- CrPC § 437 → BNSS § 480 (non-bailable offences)
- CrPC § 438 → BNSS § 483 (anticipatory bail)
- CrPC § 439 → BNSS § 484 (High Court/Sessions Court powers)
- CrPC § 436A → BNSS § 479 proviso (under-trial bail — substantially strengthened in BNSS)

Key change: BNSS § 479 proviso extends the under-trial bail provision to all offences (CrPC § 436A excluded offences punishable by death or life imprisonment from the half-sentence rule). BNSS also added a specific provision for first-time offenders.
