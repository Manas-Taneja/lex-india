## Definition

An injunction is a court order directing a party to do (mandatory injunction) or refrain from doing (prohibitory injunction) a specific act. In India, injunctions are governed by the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (§§ 36-42) and the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (Order XXXIX). Injunctions may be temporary (interlocutory) or perpetual, and are granted both in civil suits and as standalone applications.

## Current Law

**Specific Relief Act, 1963**

- **§ 36** — Preventive relief: granted at the discretion of the court by injunction — temporary or perpetual
- **§ 37** — Temporary and perpetual injunctions: temporary injunction may be granted at any stage of a suit; perpetual injunction only at the hearing and upon the merits
- **§ 38** — Perpetual injunction when granted: to prevent breach of an obligation existing in favour of the applicant; or to prevent threatened invasion of right in property or enjoyment of property
- **§ 39** — Mandatory injunctions: where it is necessary to compel performance of certain acts to prevent or reverse breach — court may grant mandatory injunction ordering affirmative action
- **§ 40** — Damages in lieu of or in addition to injunction: court may award damages in addition to, or in substitution for, an injunction
- **§ 41** — Injunction refused: injunction shall not be granted to restrain a person from instituting civil proceedings; to restrain a court or legislative body; to prevent breach of contract where money compensation is adequate; against public officers in discharge of public duties; where conduct of the applicant disentitles him to equitable relief; to stay a criminal proceeding

**Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 — Order XXXIX**

- **Rule 1** — Temporary injunction: court may grant temporary injunction to restrain a party from wasting, damaging, or alienating property; or from removing property from local limits; or from causing injury to plaintiff pending suit
- **Rule 2** — Injunction to restrain repetition or continuance of breach: in any suit for restraining defendant from committing breach, court may grant interim injunction at the commencement of the suit
- **Rule 2A** — Consequences of disobedience: if the defendant disobeys the injunction, the court may attach property, commit to civil prison, and impose fines
- **Rule 3** — Before granting injunction, court shall direct notice to opposite party (unless case of extreme urgency — then ex parte)
- **Rule 3A** — Where ex parte injunction granted, suit must be disposed of within 30 days
- **Rule 4** — Order for injunction may be discharged, varied, or set aside on cause shown

## Key Provisions

**Three-part test for temporary injunction (settled by Supreme Court in Dalpat Kumar v Prahlad Singh, 1991):**

1. **Prima facie case:** Plaintiff must show a triable issue — not that they will succeed, but that they have an arguable case
2. **Balance of convenience:** Whether greater harm would be caused by granting or refusing the injunction — whose injury is harder to remedy
3. **Irreparable harm:** Whether damages would be an inadequate remedy; whether the harm if the injunction is not granted cannot be compensated in money

All three conditions must be satisfied. Absence of any one disentitles the applicant.

**Ex parte injunction:** Granted without hearing the opposite party in cases of extreme urgency or where notice would defeat the purpose. Court must record reasons; notice must follow immediately; injunction valid for limited period until further hearing.

**Mareva injunction / freezing order:** Indian courts have recognised the equivalent of Mareva injunctions — injunctions to freeze assets pending judgment where there is risk of dissipation. Statutory basis under Order XXXIX Rule 1 (alienation/waste) read with § 151 CPC (inherent powers).

**Anton Piller equivalent (search and seizure):** In intellectual property and commercial matters, courts grant ex parte orders permitting plaintiffs to enter premises and inspect/seize evidence before the defendant can destroy it. Governed by inherent powers under § 151 CPC.

**Mandatory vs prohibitory:**

| Type | Direction | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Prohibitory | Stop doing X | Restrain from publishing defamatory article |
| Mandatory | Do X | Restore building demolished in breach of covenant |

Mandatory injunctions granted more sparingly — greater interference with defendant's rights.

**Status quo ante injunction:** To restore parties to their original position prior to the act complained of. Overlap with mandatory injunctions.

**Perpetual injunction at trial:** Granted after full merits hearing; conclusively determines the right. Can be granted independently or alongside damages/specific performance.

**Injunctions and arbitration:** Under § 9 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, parties may apply to court for interim injunctions before, during, or after arbitration. Under § 17, the arbitral tribunal itself may grant interim measures (enforceable as court orders post-2015 amendment).

**Refusal of injunction — § 41 SRA:** Injunction will not be granted to restrain a breach of contract where money compensation is an adequate remedy. Also refused when plaintiff's conduct (delay, laches, acquiescence) disentitles them in equity.
