## Definition

A limitation period is the maximum time after an event within which a party must file a legal action. After the period expires, the claim is time-barred and the court cannot grant relief even if the claim is otherwise valid. In India, limitation is governed by the Limitation Act, 1963, which prescribes periods for suits, appeals, and applications across a First Schedule of 137 articles.

## Current Law

**Limitation Act, 1963**

- **§ 2(j)** — "Period of limitation" means the period prescribed under the Schedule for any suit/appeal/application
- **§ 3** — Bar of limitation: every suit instituted, appeal preferred, or application made after the period of limitation shall be dismissed, even if limitation is not pleaded as a defence
- **§ 5** — Extension of prescribed period (appeals/applications only): court may admit if appellant/applicant satisfies the court that there was sufficient cause for not filing within the period. Does not apply to original suits
- **§ 6** — Legal disability: minor, insane person, or idiot — limitation does not run during disability; fresh period runs after disability ceases
- **§ 8** — Maximum disability extension: limitation cannot be extended by more than 3 years on account of disability
- **§ 12** — Exclusion of time in legal proceedings: time during which the former suit/proceeding was pending in a court is excluded when computing limitation for a fresh suit
- **§ 14** — Exclusion of time of proceeding bona fide in court without jurisdiction: if a suit was prosecuted in good faith in a court that had no jurisdiction, that time is excluded
- **§ 15** — Exclusion of time when defendant absent from India or conceals himself
- **§ 17** — Effect of fraud or mistake: where suit/application based on fraud, or where mistake discovered — period runs from when fraud/mistake discovered or could with due diligence have been discovered
- **§ 18** — Effect of acknowledgement: if defendant acknowledges liability in writing signed before expiry of limitation — fresh period runs from date of acknowledgement
- **§ 19** — Effect of payment: part-payment on account of a debt before expiry — fresh period runs from date of payment
- **§ 21** — Effect of substitution of plaintiff: where a new plaintiff is substituted after limitation has expired, limitation is deemed to have expired against the new plaintiff (unless relation back applies)
- **§ 27** — Extinguishment of right to property: title extinguished at expiry of period to sue for recovery of immovable property (adverse possession effect)
- **First Schedule** — Articles 1-137: specific periods for each type of suit, appeal, or application

**Key periods (First Schedule):**

## Key Provisions

**Limitation bars the remedy, not the right:** A time-barred claim is extinguished as a right of action in court, but the underlying right may survive. Exception: immovable property suits — § 27 extinguishes title after 12 years of adverse possession.

**Acknowledgement (§ 18) — requirements:**
1. Writing
2. Signed by the party liable
3. Before expiry of original period
4. Unambiguous admission of liability (need not state exact amount)
5. Fresh period runs from acknowledgement date (same period as original)

**Part-payment (§ 19):** Any payment on account of principal/interest on a debt restarts the limitation clock. Does not require the debtor to sign; the creditor must prove payment.

**§ 5 condonation — "sufficient cause":** Courts apply a liberal standard — bona fide mistake, illness, natural calamity, incorrect legal advice, government delay. But the explanation must cover the entire period of delay.

**Adverse possession (§ 27 + Art 65):** If a trespasser is in peaceful, open, continuous, and hostile possession of immovable property for 12 years (private) / 30 years (government), the original owner's title is extinguished. Post-2014, courts have restricted adverse possession claims that reward trespassers — but the statutory provision remains.

**Fraud/concealment (§ 17):** Limitation does not run when the plaintiff could not reasonably have discovered the fraud or mistake. Constructive discovery — when the plaintiff ought to have known with due diligence — starts the clock.

**Limitation in arbitration:** The Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (§ 43) provides that the Limitation Act applies to arbitration as it applies to proceedings in court. Limitation for filing arbitration is governed by the First Schedule (usually 3 years from accrual of cause of action).

**Limitation in criminal law:** The BNSS (§ 468-473) prescribes limitation for taking cognizance of offences: 6 months for offences punishable with fine only; 1 year for offences punishable up to 1 year; 3 years for offences punishable up to 3 years. No limitation for offences punishable with more than 3 years.
