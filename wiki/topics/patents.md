# Patents

## Definition
A patent is a statutory monopoly granted to an inventor for a limited term in exchange for full public disclosure of the invention. India's patent law is governed by the Patents Act 1970, fundamentally reformed by the 2005 amendment which introduced product patents for food, pharmaceuticals, and chemicals to comply with the TRIPS Agreement.

## Current Law

### Patents Act 1970 (as amended 2005)

- § 2(1)(j) — "invention": a new product or process involving an inventive step and capable of industrial application
- § 2(1)(ja) — "inventive step": a feature that makes the invention not obvious to a person skilled in the art, having regard to prior art
- § 2(1)(ac) — "capable of industrial application": the invention can be made or used in some industry
- § 3 — non-patentable inventions: discoveries of natural phenomena, abstract theories, mathematical methods, business methods, mental acts, literary/artistic works, methods of treatment of humans/animals, plants/animals (other than microorganisms), traditional knowledge (§ 3(p))
- § 3(d) — (Novartis Glivec ruling, Supreme Court 2013): mere discovery of a new form of a known substance which does not result in enhancement of known efficacy is not patentable; salts, esters, polymorphs, isomers require proof of significantly enhanced efficacy
- § 6 — persons entitled to apply: true and first inventor, assignee of inventor, or legal representative
- § 11A — application published 18 months from filing date (or priority date); early publication possible on request
- § 13 — examination by patent examiner; first examination report (FER) issued within 6 months of request for examination
- § 25(1) — pre-grant opposition: any person may oppose after publication and before grant on specified grounds
- § 25(2) — post-grant opposition: any interested person within 12 months of grant
- § 48 — rights of patentee: exclusive right to prevent others from making, using, offering for sale, selling, or importing the patented product/process
- § 53 — term of patent: 20 years from the date of filing the application
- § 68 — assignment of patent: must be in writing and registered; unregistered assignments void against subsequent assignees
- § 84 — compulsory licence: any person may apply after 3 years from grant if (a) reasonable requirements of public not satisfied, (b) not available at reasonably affordable price, or (c) not worked in India
- § 92 — compulsory licence in national emergency or extreme urgency: Central Government may issue
- § 92A — compulsory licence for export to least-developed/developing countries lacking manufacturing capacity (TRIPS Art. 31bis)
- § 100 — use of patented inventions for purposes of government without consent; reasonable compensation payable
- § 104A — reversal of burden of proof in process patent infringement suits: defendant must prove independent discovery if product is identical

### Form 27 — Working Statement

- Patentee and licensee must file annual statement of working (Form 27) with the Patent Office within 3 months of the end of each financial year
- Non-filing is an offence punishable with fine up to ₹10 lakh

### PCT — Indian National Phase

- India is a member of the Patent Cooperation Treaty
- International applications enter Indian national phase within 31 months from priority date
- Filed before the Indian Patent Office acting as national/designated office

## Key Provisions

### Grounds for Pre-Grant and Post-Grant Opposition (§ 25)

| Ground | Pre-grant § 25(1) | Post-grant § 25(2) |
|---|---|---|
| Anticipation by prior publication | Yes | Yes |
| Prior claim in India | Yes | Yes |
| Prior use/sale in India | Yes | Yes |
| Obviousness / lack of inventive step | Yes | Yes |
| Non-patentable subject matter (§ 3/§ 4) | Yes | Yes |
| Insufficiency of disclosure | Yes | Yes |
| False suggestion or misrepresentation | Yes | Yes |

### Compulsory Licence Conditions (§ 84)

| Criterion | Detail |
|---|---|
| Waiting period | 3 years from date of grant |
| Grounds | Unreasonable requirements; unaffordable price; not worked in India |
| Remuneration | Reasonable royalty fixed by Controller |
| Leading case | Natco Pharma v Bayer (2012) — first compulsory licence granted in India |

### Appeal Hierarchy

| Forum | Jurisdiction |
|---|---|
| Controller General of Patents | First instance decisions, oppositions |
| High Court | Appeals from Controller (IPAB abolished 2021); revocation petitions |
| Supreme Court | Further appeals on questions of law |
