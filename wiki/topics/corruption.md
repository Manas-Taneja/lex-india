## Definition

Corruption offences in India are primarily governed by the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (PC Act), amended significantly in 2018. The Act criminalises bribery of public servants, acceptance of undue advantage, and possession of disproportionate assets. The 2018 amendment for the first time also criminalised the bribe-giver (not just the receiver).

## Current Law

**Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (as amended in 2018)**

- **§ 2(c)** — Public servant: broad definition including government employees, employees of public sector undertakings, judges, arbitrators, persons authorised to perform public duties
- **§ 7** — Public servant taking undue advantage: imprisonment 3–7 years + fine
- **§ 7A** — Taking undue advantage to influence a public servant (bribe-giver): imprisonment 3–7 years + fine *(inserted by 2018 amendment)*
- **§ 8** — Public servant abetting offence under § 7A: imprisonment 3–7 years + fine
- **§ 9** — Commercial organisations bribing public servants: fine (with provision for officer liability)
- **§ 11** — Public servant obtaining undue advantage without consideration from persons involved in proceedings: imprisonment 6 months–5 years + fine
- **§ 12** — Abetment of offences defined in this Act: same punishment as the principal offence
- **§ 13** — Criminal misconduct by a public servant: disproportionate assets, or habitual acceptance of gratification — imprisonment 4–10 years + fine
- **§ 17A** — Prior sanction required before investigation of a public servant for acts done in discharge of official functions *(inserted by 2018 amendment)*
- **§ 19** — Previous sanction of competent authority required for prosecution of a public servant
- **§ 20** — Presumption of guilt where acceptance of undue advantage proved

**BNS, 2023:**
- **§ 61** — Criminal conspiracy (applicable to corruption rings)

## Key Provisions

**Undue advantage (§ 2(d)):** Any gratification (monetary or non-monetary) other than legal remuneration. Includes money, gifts, employment, contracts, favours.

**2018 Amendment — key changes:**
1. Bribe-giver now criminally liable (§ 7A) — earlier only the receiver was liable
2. Exception: Person compelled to give bribe and who reports within 7 days to law enforcement is not liable
3. Prior approval (§ 17A) required from competent authority before investigation of sitting/former public servants for acts done in discharge of official duties — addresses concern of political misuse

**Sanction for prosecution (§ 19):** Required from the employer authority before a public servant can be prosecuted. Courts have held that defective sanction is curable if no prejudice is caused.

**Disproportionate assets (§ 13(1)(b)):** Assets held by a public servant or on their behalf that are disproportionate to known sources of income. The accused must explain the source; failure to explain raises presumption of corruption.

**Investigation agency:** The Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI) investigates cases against Central Government servants. State anti-corruption bureaus/vigilance departments handle State government servants.

**Lokpal and Lokayukta:**
- Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 — independent ombudsmen for complaints against public servants
- Lokpal: Central level (covers Prime Minister subject to conditions, Ministers, MPs, Group A-C officers)
- Lokayuktas: State level (legislation varies by State)
