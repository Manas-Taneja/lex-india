## Definition

Arbitration is a private, binding dispute resolution mechanism where parties agree to have their dispute decided by one or more arbitrators instead of a court. In India, it is governed by the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (A&C Act), which is based on the UNCITRAL Model Law. The Act has been significantly amended in 2015, 2019, and 2021 to speed up proceedings and promote institutional arbitration.

## Current Law

**Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (as amended 2015, 2019, 2021)**

- **§ 7** — Arbitration agreement: must be in writing; can be a separate agreement or clause in a contract; covers disputes relating to a defined legal relationship
- **§ 8** — Reference to arbitration by court: if a party raises an arbitration agreement before filing a written statement, court must refer the dispute to arbitration
- **§ 9** — Interim measures by court: parties may apply to court for interim relief before or during arbitration (appointment of receiver, injunction, preservation of property)
- **§ 11** — Appointment of arbitrator: if parties fail to agree, High Court (international commercial) or designated authority (domestic) appoints; Supreme Court or High Court designates arbitral institutions *(2019 amendment)*
- **§ 12** — Grounds for challenge of arbitrator: lack of independence/impartiality; Fifth Schedule lists relationships that give rise to justifiable doubts; Seventh Schedule lists absolute ineligibility
- **§ 16** — Kompetenz-Kompetenz: arbitral tribunal rules on its own jurisdiction; challenge to jurisdiction must be raised no later than the first statement on substance
- **§ 17** — Interim measures by arbitral tribunal *(strengthened by 2015 amendment)*: tribunal may order interim measures; order is enforceable as a court order
- **§ 23** — Statement of claim and defence: parties exchange pleadings; tribunal sets procedural timetable
- **§ 24** — Hearings: oral or document-only; parties entitled to be heard
- **§ 28** — Rules applicable to substance: parties choose governing law; failing that — law most appropriate to dispute (international) or Indian substantive law (domestic)
- **§ 29A** — Time limit for arbitral award *(inserted 2015)*: award must be made within 12 months from constitution of tribunal; extendable by 6 months by party consent; beyond 18 months — court approval required; mandate terminates if award not made in time (unless extended)
- **§ 29B** — Fast-track arbitration: by agreement of parties; single arbitrator; only written submissions; award within 6 months
- **§ 31** — Form and content of arbitral award: must be in writing, signed, reasoned (unless agreed otherwise), dated, and specify place of arbitration
- **§ 34** — Setting aside arbitral award: application to court; grounds limited — incapacity of party, invalid arbitration agreement, no notice of proceedings, award beyond scope, composition of tribunal contrary to agreement, dispute not arbitrable, or conflict with public policy of India *(Explanation 2 — patent illegality applies only to domestic awards)*
- **§ 36** — Enforcement: award enforced as a decree after § 34 period expires or challenge dismissed
- **§ 37** — Appealable orders: appeal from § 34 order; also from §§ 9, 16, 17 orders — no appeal from interim order during arbitration
- **§ 44-60** — Foreign awards (New York Convention): enforcement of awards from New York Convention countries; grounds for refusal similar to § 34 but narrower; no patent illegality ground for foreign awards
- **§ 62-86** — Conciliation: voluntary; conciliator facilitates settlement; settlement agreement is binding and enforceable as court decree

## Key Provisions

**Arbitrability:** Not all disputes can be arbitrated. Non-arbitrable disputes include: criminal matters, matrimonial relief, insolvency, probate, anti-competition matters, and consumer disputes (where consumer opts for consumer forum). Landlord-tenant evictions governed by rent control acts — generally not arbitrable.

**Public policy (§ 34 Explanation 1):** Award can be set aside if it is contrary to: (a) fundamental policy of Indian law; (b) basic notions of morality or justice; (c) interests of India. Courts narrowed this post-2015 — mere legal error not sufficient; must be a fundamental legal flaw.

**Patent illegality (§ 34 Explanation 2):** Only for domestic awards: award may be set aside if it is patently illegal on the face of the record (not involving just re-appreciation of evidence).

**Severability:** If only a part of the award deals with disputes not submitted to arbitration, only that part is set aside (§ 34(2)(a)(iv)).

**MSME arbitration councils:** Under the MSMED Act, disputes between MSME suppliers and buyers go to the Micro and Small Enterprise Facilitation Council before arbitration.

**Institutional vs ad hoc arbitration:** 2019 amendment introduced accreditation of arbitral institutions by an Arbitration Council of India (ACI). Major institutions — DIAC (Delhi), Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration (MCIA), ICC India, LCIA India. Institutional arbitration preferred for international disputes.

**Emergency arbitrators:** Major institutions have emergency arbitrator provisions — interim relief before full tribunal constituted. No express statutory basis in A&C Act; enforcement of emergency orders through § 9.

**Section 8 mandatory reference:** If there is an arbitration agreement and the applicant raises it before filing the written statement, the court has no discretion — must refer to arbitration. Exception: subject matter is non-arbitrable.
