## Definition

Cheating is the act of deceiving a person fraudulently or dishonestly, inducing them to deliver property or to do or omit something they would not do if not deceived, causing damage or harm to their body, mind, reputation, or property. Deception is the core element — without it, there is no cheating.

## Current Law

**Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS)**

- **§ 316** — Definition of cheating
- **§ 317** — Cheating with knowledge that wrongful loss will result (up to 1 year or fine or both)
- **§ 318** — Cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property (up to 7 years + fine)
- **§ 319** — Cheating by personation (up to 5 years + fine)

## Key Provisions

**Elements of cheating (§ 316):**
1. Deception (false representation, concealment, or false pretence)
2. Fraudulent or dishonest inducement
3. The victim delivers property **or** does/omits an act
4. The act causes or is likely to cause damage/harm

**§ 318 (aggravated cheating — the main provision):** Applies when the cheating involves dishonestly inducing delivery of property or causing alteration/destruction of a valuable document. Carries up to 7 years imprisonment + fine.

**§ 318(4):** Specifically covers cheating by making false claims or representations — this absorbed the old IPC § 420 (cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property). Punishment: up to 7 years + fine.

**Personation (§ 319):** Impersonating another person, living or dead, to deceive — carries up to 5 years.

## Transition Note

Previously governed by **Indian Penal Code, 1860 (IPC)**:
- IPC § 415 → BNS § 316 (definition of cheating)
- IPC § 417 → BNS § 317 (punishment for basic cheating)
- IPC § 420 → BNS § 318(4) (cheating and inducing delivery — absorbed into § 318)
- IPC § 416 → BNS § 319 (cheating by personation)

IPC § 420 was the most commonly invoked cheating section ("420 case" is colloquial for fraud). In BNS, this is now § 318(4) — same offence, different numbering.
