## Definition

Money laundering is the process of concealing the origins of illegally obtained proceeds (proceeds of crime) by passing them through a complex sequence of banking transfers, shell companies, or other transactions to make them appear legitimate. The Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002 (PMLA) criminalises the act and provides for attachment and confiscation of proceeds of crime.

## Current Law

**Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002 (PMLA)**

- **§ 2(u)** — Proceeds of crime: property derived from a scheduled offence, or the value of such property
- **§ 3** — Offence of money laundering: whosoever directly or indirectly attempts to indulge or knowingly assists or is a party to, or is actually involved in any process or activity connected with the proceeds of crime and projecting it as untainted property
- **§ 4** — Punishment: rigorous imprisonment 3–7 years + fine up to ₹5 lakh; for offences under Part A Paragraph 2 of Schedule (narcotics): up to 10 years
- **§ 5** — Attachment of property: Enforcement Directorate (ED) can provisionally attach proceeds of crime for up to 180 days
- **§ 8** — Adjudication by Adjudicating Authority — confirms or releases attachment
- **§ 17** — Search and seizure by ED officers
- **§ 19** — Power of arrest by ED officers
- **§ 24** — Burden of proof: where a person is accused of money laundering, the burden of proving that the proceeds are not proceeds of crime is on the accused *(reverse burden)*
- **§ 44** — Special courts (designated Sessions Courts) for trial of PMLA offences
- **§ 45** — Bail conditions: no person accused of an offence punishable for 3+ years under Part A of the Schedule shall be released on bail unless the court is satisfied that (a) there are reasonable grounds for believing the accused is not guilty, AND (b) the accused will not commit any offence while on bail *(twin conditions)*

## Key Provisions

**Scheduled offences (Schedule to PMLA):** Money laundering requires a predicate (underlying) offence. The Schedule lists predicate offences — includes corruption, drug trafficking, terrorism, human trafficking, counterfeiting, tax evasion, cybercrimes, and many others. Over 400 offences listed.

**Process of money laundering (three-stage model):**
1. **Placement** — Introducing illegal funds into the financial system
2. **Layering** — Obscuring the trail (multiple transfers, shell companies, foreign accounts)
3. **Integration** — Re-introducing laundered money as apparently legitimate income

**Enforcement Directorate (ED):** Central agency under the Ministry of Finance responsible for PMLA enforcement. Independent of CBI and State police for PMLA matters.

**Section 24 — Reverse burden:** Once the ED proves possession of proceeds of crime, the accused must prove innocence. Upheld by Supreme Court in Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v Union of India (2022).

**Section 45 — Twin bail conditions:** Extremely high bar for bail. Accused must satisfy the court on both conditions. Supreme Court has held these conditions are valid (Vijay Madanlal Choudhary, 2022).

**PMLA vs BNS:** PMLA is a special law. Being accused of a scheduled offence does not automatically mean PMLA applies — the ED must separately establish that the accused has dealt with proceeds of crime. Both charges can coexist.

**Reporting obligations:**
- Banks, financial institutions, intermediaries, and certain professionals (chartered accountants, company secretaries, lawyers) are required to file Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) with the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-India)
- KYC norms under PMLA Rules apply to all regulated entities
