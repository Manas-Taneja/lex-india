# Muslim Personal Law

## Definition
Muslim personal law governs the family and succession affairs of Muslims in India primarily through classical Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh), as applied by the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937. It covers marriage, divorce, mahr, maintenance, inheritance, and wakf endowments.

## Current Law

### Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937
- § 2 — Notwithstanding any custom or usage to the contrary, Shariat (Islamic personal law) shall apply in all questions regarding intestate succession, special property of females, personal status, marriage, dissolution of marriage, maintenance, dower, guardianship, gift, wakf, and pre-emption to persons professing Islam

### Marriage (Nikah)
- A civil contract requiring: offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) in one sitting; presence of two male witnesses (or one male and two female witnesses under Hanafi school); both parties competent (sane, adult, or near-puberty)
- Mahr (dower) is a mandatory payment or promise from husband to wife; an independent financial right of the wife
  - Prompt mahr (mu'ajjal): payable immediately on demand
  - Deferred mahr (muwajjal): payable on dissolution of marriage or death
- Polygamy: a Muslim male may have up to four wives simultaneously, subject to ability to treat them with equal justice (Quran 4:3); first wife's consent is not a legal prerequisite under Indian law (contrast with HMA § 5)

### Divorce (Talaq)
| Form | Description | Validity under Indian Law |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Talaq-e-ahsan | Single revocable pronouncement; 3-month iddat; most approved form | Valid |
| Talaq-e-hasan | Three pronouncements in three successive months | Valid |
| Talaq-e-biddat (triple talaq) | Three pronouncements in one sitting; instantaneous | Void (MWPRM Act 2019 § 3; SC Shayara Bano 2017) |
| Khula | Wife-initiated dissolution; wife returns mahr; requires husband's agreement | Valid |
| Mubarat | Mutual agreement to dissolve marriage | Valid |
| Judicial divorce | By court under Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939 | Valid (statutory grounds) |

### Shayara Bano v Union of India (SC 2017)
Five-judge constitutional bench (3:2) struck down instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) as unconstitutional — being manifestly arbitrary and violative of Articles 14 and 25. The majority held it was not an essential religious practice. Parliament codified this in the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019.

### Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019
- § 3 — Talaq-e-biddat (instantaneous triple talaq by spoken, written, or electronic means) is void and illegal
- § 4 — Husband who pronounces such talaq is liable for imprisonment up to 3 years and fine (cognisable, non-bailable offence)
- § 5 — Divorced woman (and her dependent children) entitled to subsistence allowance from husband as determined by magistrate
- § 7 — Offence compoundable at wife's instance; magistrate may bail husband during compounding stage

### Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939
- § 2 — Grounds on which a Muslim wife may obtain a decree for dissolution of marriage:
  - Husband's whereabouts unknown for four years
  - Husband sentenced to imprisonment for seven or more years
  - Husband failing to perform marital obligations for three years
  - Husband impotent at time of marriage and continues to be so
  - Husband of unsound mind for two years, or suffering from virulent venereal disease, or leprosy
  - Wife was given in marriage before 15 and repudiates it before 18 (no consummation required)
  - Husband treating her with cruelty (habitual assault, association with women of ill repute, compelling her to lead immoral life, disposing of her property, obstructing religious practice, treating co-wives unequally)

### Iddat (Waiting Period)
| Situation | Duration |
|-----------|----------|
| Divorce (menstruating wife) | Three menstrual cycles (quru) |
| Divorce (non-menstruating or post-menopausal) | Three lunar months |
| Husband's death | Four months and ten days (130 days) |
| Divorce during pregnancy | Until delivery |

Purposes: determining paternity, allowing for reconciliation, ensuring husband's maintenance obligation (Danial Latifi: provision must be for future life, not merely iddat period).

### Mahr and Maintenance
- Mahr is an independent right; non-payment allows wife to refuse cohabitation
- Post-divorce maintenance: SC in Danial Latifi v Union of India (2001) held Muslim Women Act 1986 requires husband to make "reasonable and fair provision" for entire future of divorced wife; one-time lump sum must reflect future needs, not just iddat subsistence
- Shah Bano (SC 1985): granted maintenance under CrPC § 125 to divorced Muslim woman; led to 1986 Act; effectively restored by Danial Latifi and subsequent decisions

## Key Provisions

### Wakf
- Wakf: permanent dedication of property for religious or charitable purposes as defined by Muslim law
- Governed by Wakf Act 1995 (as amended 2013; Wakf Amendment Act 2025 pending/passed at time of writing)
- Wakf Board in each state administers and manages wakf properties

### Succession under Muslim Law
| Principle | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Quranic heirs (Sharers) | Fixed fractions: Quran 4:11-12 |
| Residuaries (Asaba) | Take residue after sharers |
| Bequest (Wasiyat) | Max 1/3 of estate; cannot be to an heir without other heirs' consent |
| Male:female ratio | 2:1 in corresponding categories |
| Non-Muslim heirs | Cannot inherit from Muslim under Hanafi law |
