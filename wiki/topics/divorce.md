## Definition

Divorce is the legal dissolution of a valid marriage by a court. In India, personal law governs divorce — Hindus are governed by the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955; Muslims by personal law (Muslim Personal Law Application Act, 1937); Christians by the Divorce Act, 1869; Parsis by the Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act, 1936; all others (or parties choosing secular law) by the Special Marriage Act, 1954.

This page covers divorce under the **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (HMA)**, which applies to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs.

## Current Law

**Hindu Marriage Act, 1955**

- **§ 13** — Grounds for divorce (petition by either party)
- **§ 13A** — Alternate relief in divorce proceedings (judicial separation instead of divorce)
- **§ 13B** — Divorce by mutual consent
- **§ 14** — No petition within 1 year of marriage
- **§ 25** — Permanent alimony and maintenance
- **§ 26** — Custody of children
- **§ 27** — Disposal of property

## Key Provisions

**Grounds for divorce (§ 13):**
1. Adultery
2. Cruelty (physical or mental)
3. Desertion (2+ years)
4. Conversion to another religion
5. Unsoundness of mind / mental disorder (incurable)
6. Leprosy (virulent and incurable) — *Note: constitutional validity questioned*
7. Venereal disease (communicable form)
8. Renunciation of the world (sanyas)
9. Not heard of for 7+ years (presumed dead)

**Additional grounds available only to wife (§ 13(2)):**
- Husband has another wife living at time of marriage (bigamy)
- Husband guilty of rape, sodomy, or bestiality
- Non-resumption of cohabitation after maintenance decree (2 years)
- Marriage solemnised before age 15 (right to repudiate on turning 18)

**Mutual consent divorce (§ 13B):**
- Both parties file joint petition
- Separation of at least 1 year required
- Second motion after 6 months (cooling-off period) — Supreme Court can waive this in exceptional cases (Amardeep Singh v. Harveen Kaur, 2017)
- Court satisfied that consent is genuine and not under pressure

**Irretrievable breakdown (not yet statutory):** Supreme Court has inherent power under Article 142 to dissolve marriage on ground of irretrievable breakdown (Shilpa Sailesh v. Varun Sreenivasan, 2023 — 5-judge bench). The Marriage Laws (Amendment) Bill proposing to add this ground has not yet been enacted.

**Alimony (§ 25):** Court may order permanent alimony payable by either spouse. Interim maintenance under § 24 during pendency of proceedings. Amount based on status, needs, and conduct of parties.

**Child custody (§ 26):** Court makes orders for custody, maintenance, and education of minor children, keeping the welfare of the child as the paramount consideration.
