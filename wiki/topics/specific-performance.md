## Definition

Specific performance is an equitable remedy that compels a party to perform a contract as agreed, rather than merely paying damages. In India, it is governed by the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (SRA), as substantially amended by the Specific Relief (Amendment) Act, 2018. The 2018 amendment changed specific performance from a discretionary remedy to a general right — courts must ordinarily grant it unless statutory exceptions apply.

## Current Law

**Specific Relief Act, 1963 (as amended 2018)**

- **§ 10** — Specific performance of contracts: contracts for transfer of immovable property, and contracts where compensation in money is not an adequate relief, shall be specifically enforced. The 2018 amendment removed the word "may" and replaced it with "shall" — performance is now mandatory, not discretionary
- **§ 11** — Specific performance of part of contract: if part of the contract cannot be performed, court may enforce the part that can be, with compensation for the rest
- **§ 14** — Contracts not specifically enforceable: (a) where compensation is adequate; (b) contracts for personal service; (c) contracts depending on personal qualifications; (d) contracts which by their nature are determinable (revocable at will)
- **§ 14A** — Infrastructure contracts *(inserted 2018)*: courts shall not grant injunctions that impede or delay infrastructure projects; technical experts may be appointed
- **§ 15** — Who may obtain specific performance: parties to the contract and their legal heirs/assigns
- **§ 16** — Personal bars to relief: specific performance not granted against a person who has obtained the contract by fraud, misrepresentation, or undue influence; or who is otherwise unable or unwilling to perform their part
- **§ 20** — Substituted performance *(inserted 2018)*: aggrieved party may have the contract performed by a third party and recover costs from the defaulting party — an alternative to seeking specific performance in court
- **§ 21** — Power to award compensation: court may award compensation in addition to or instead of specific performance
- **§ 22** — Power to grant relief for possession, partition, etc.: in a suit for specific performance, court may also order delivery of possession of property, partition, or refund of earnest money
- **§ 23** — Liquidated damages not a bar: existence of a liquidated damages clause does not bar specific performance
- **§ 34** — Declaratory decrees: person entitled to any legal character or to any right over property may seek declaration from court; defendant denying such title is required
- **§ 36-38** — Preventive relief (injunctions): temporary and perpetual injunctions; injunction to prevent breach of contract or tort
- **§ 39** — Mandatory injunctions: court may direct performance of certain acts to prevent or to reverse irreversible harm

## Key Provisions

**2018 amendment — shift from discretion to right:**

Pre-2018: courts had wide discretion to award damages instead of specific performance. Post-2018: if the contract is one that should be specifically enforced (§ 10), the court must grant it. Discretion to refuse exists only where a § 14 exception applies or the plaintiff is personally barred (§ 16).

**Contracts not enforceable (§ 14):**

| Type | Reason |
|------|--------|
| Personal service/employment | Cannot compel human labour |
| Determinable contracts | Revocable at will — no point enforcing |
| Contracts requiring constant court supervision | Impractical |
| Where compensation is adequate | Equity acts only where damages insufficient |

**Substituted performance (§ 20):** Aggrieved party gives written notice → 30 days for defaulting party to cure → if uncured, aggrieved party performs through third party → recovers reasonable costs. This route does not require filing a suit — out-of-court remedy. Pursuing substituted performance does not bar a subsequent damages suit, but bars a specific performance suit.

**Limitation:** Suit for specific performance of a contract to transfer immovable property — 3 years from the date fixed for performance or, if no date fixed, when plaintiff has notice that performance is refused (Art. 54, Limitation Act).

**Earnest money forfeiture vs specific performance:** Retention of earnest money by vendor on breach by purchaser is a separate right. It does not bar specific performance by the purchaser if the purchaser is ready and willing to perform.

**Ready and willing to perform:** Plaintiff must plead and prove that they were always ready and willing to perform their part of the contract. Failure to aver readiness and willingness is fatal to the suit.

**Infrastructure projects (§ 14A):** No injunction shall be granted against infrastructure projects (roads, railways, power, ports, telecommunications) as designated by government. Expert committee of technical experts may be constituted to resolve disputes without halting the project.
